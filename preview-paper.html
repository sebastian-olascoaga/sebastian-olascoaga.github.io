Field experiment · 2024

When buyers see a home's flood risk, where do they go instead?

Daryl Fairweather · Matthew E. Kahn · Robert D. Metcalfe · Sebastian Sandoval Olascoaga

Revise & Resubmit · American Economic Review

In the first nationwide field experiment of climate-risk disclosure in the U.S. housing market, 17.5 million Redfin users were randomly shown — or not shown — the flood risk of every home they viewed. Among those already shopping in high-risk areas, treated buyers closed on properties with 59% less flood exposure. The market repriced. Republican and Democratic counties responded the same.

Why it matters

Climate change is rewriting where Americans can safely live. The housing market hasn't been pricing it.

Climate-driven flood risk in the United States is rising. But housing markets have historically been quiet about it: buyers often don't know what they're buying, lenders don't always price it, and home prices in exposed neighborhoods don't reliably reflect the science of where the water is going.

The standard policy lever is mandated disclosure — require sellers to tell buyers about risk. This experiment tested a weaker but more deployable cousin: just show people the number, at the moment they're choosing. If buyers act on it, disclosure has bite. If they don't, the policy needs reinforcement elsewhere.

How we did it

A covert, nationwide field experiment on Redfin's actual platform.

From October 1, 2020 through January 3, 2021, Redfin's randomization framework silently split 17.5 million users into treatment (a First Street Foundation Flood Factor score, 1–10, appeared on every property page) and control (the standard listing). Sellers were blind. Agents were blind. Users weren't notified — the information was just there.

We tracked the same individuals across every step of the funnel — search, tour, offer, close — and linked the platform behavior to MLS transactions. Identification rests on three estimators: an average-treatment difference-in-differences, a conditional-average-treatment-effect interacted with each user's baseline search risk-category, and a dynamic event-study. Standard errors clustered at the user level, with Romano-Wolf multiple-testing corrections (1,000 bootstrap resamples for search, 3,000 for offers). The hedonic price analysis uses random variation in the share of treated viewers per listing as an exogenous exposure measure — a Crépon-style design applied to homes rather than labor markets.

What we found

Same people. Same budget. Different homes — and the market itself moved.

Search. Treated registered users who had been browsing the riskiest properties shifted toward homes with 11.8% less flood risk by week 9 (p<0.01). Effects grew over time; pre-trends were flat (joint test p=0.66).

Bids and closes. Those same users placed offers on homes with 57.1% less flood-score exposure (s.e. 19.73) than control buyers, and closed on homes with 58.8% less (p<0.01). Crucially, treated users weren't less likely to bid — they were just bidding on different homes.

Trade-offs. Treated high-risk buyers were 39.6% less likely to bid on waterfront properties and accepted 14.6% less square footage. They didn't adjust price or bedrooms — they traded walkability and the water view for safer ground.

The market repriced. Scaling random viewer-exposure intensity from 0 to 100% within Redfin, high-flood-risk non-waterfront homes lost ~$6,800 (1.7%) in sale price; low-risk homes gained about $4,000. Linearly extrapolated to full-market saturation: a ~$85,000 (21%) price reduction on high-risk properties. Investors responded too — moving from 0→100% treated raised the probability that a low-risk property was bought by an institutional investor by 2.5 percentage points over a 9.76% baseline.

And the political surprise. "We cannot reject the null that Biden and Trump's counties respond to the flood score in the same way for both searches and offers." A pre-registered hypothesis that the polarization literature predicted would split — didn't.
