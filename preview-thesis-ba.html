Bachelor's thesis · CIDE Mexico · 2012

The Mexican credit gap is not rejection. It's the door nobody opens.

Sebastián Sandoval Olascoaga · advised by Dra. Janina V. León Castillo

Licenciatura en Economía · 51 pp · written in Spanish

Of 24,793 Mexican microentrepreneurs surveyed in ENAMIN 2008, only 20% even applied for a loan — and of those who applied, 89% were approved. Rejected applicants and never-applicants look statistically identical on observable traits. The binding constraint isn't the bank's screen. It's the door.

The question

Why does Mexico's credit-to-GDP ratio sit near 25% — half of Brazil, a third of Chile, a quarter of the U.S.?

By 2010, credit to the private sector in Mexico was about 24.6% of GDP. Brazil sat at 56.6%; Chile at 86.3%. After the 1994–95 peso crisis, Mexican corporate credit collapsed and never recovered to pre-crisis levels; household credit, meanwhile, climbed steadily. The empirical question is what's binding: are banks rationing too tightly, or are firms not asking?

This thesis built an original two-stage theoretical model and ran a Heckman-corrected empirical test on Mexico's national microenterprise survey to find out.

How we did it

A decision model where Stage 1 is the entrepreneur and Stage 2 is the lender.

The model. A microentrepreneur first chooses whether to apply for credit, weighing a fixed application cost, a "collateralization cost" T/C, the risk-free rate, a borrower-specific risk premium P(c), and a success probability π(c) that depends on personal/vocational/financial traits. The lender then decides whether to approve. The first-order condition: apply when expected marginal revenue from credit equals expected marginal cost. The thesis derives comparative statics on wages, the risk-free rate, and borrower characteristics.

The data and estimator. ENAMIN 2008 (Encuesta Nacional de Micronegocios, INEGI), a nationally representative cross-section of Mexican micro-businesses with up to 6 employees (16 for manufacturing). Working sample: 24,793 microenterprises, of which 4,968 applied and 4,423 received. The empirical strategy is a Heckman bivariate probit with sample selection — necessary because Stage 2 is only observed for the 4,968 firms that applied in Stage 1. The estimated residual correlation Rho ≈ −0.66 to −0.85 (significant at 1%) confirms the selection problem is empirically real, not just textbook concern.

What it shows

Two traits move the lender. Almost everything else moves the applicant.

The lender's screen is narrow. After Heckman correction, the strongest robust predictor of approval is being female (coefficient ≈ −0.20 to −0.29 on male in Stage 2; significant at 1%) — consistent with the microfinance literature on female repayment rates and with Mexico's targeted lending programs for women. The other significant predictor: being in the top income quintile (coefficient ≈ +0.18 in Heckman Stage 2). Capital value looks significant in a naive Probit but disappears once selection is corrected.

The applicant's filter is everything else. Industry mix shapes who applies: commerce raises the probability of applying (+0.25, p<0.01); construction strongly reduces it (−0.49 to −0.54, p<0.01). And critically, when you compare the 19,825 microentrepreneurs who never applied to the 545 who applied and were rejected, the two groups are statistically indistinguishable on observable traits. They self-exclude because they correctly anticipate they would fail — the canonical "discouraged borrower" (Japelli 1990).

Si el mercado crediticio fuera eficiente y perfecto, el dinero debería de fluir de ahorradores ricos a empresarios pobres. — from the thesis · "If the credit market were efficient and perfect, money should flow from rich savers to poor entrepreneurs."

Context

Written at 22. Cited the J-PAL frontier.

The thesis was submitted in October 2012 when Sebastián was 21–22. That same cohort he earned the highest national score on Mexico's CENEVAL standardized economics exit examination. The bibliography spans the cutting-edge RCT microcredit literature of the moment (Banerjee–Duflo 2010, Karlan–Zinman 2010/2011, Crepón–Devoto–Duflo–Parienté 2011) alongside the classical theoretical canon (Stiglitz & Weiss 1981, Bester 1985, Japelli 1990, Levine 1997).

The methodological habit — specify a theoretical decision model first, then estimate it with the appropriate selection correction on real population data — runs continuously through to his later evaluation and program-impact work. The substantive concern — who gets filtered out at the door, even when the institution would accept them — turns up again in his JAMA Network Open paper on eviction policy, where the mechanism is community spillover rather than personal access, and in his City of Boston work on which residents take up city services.
