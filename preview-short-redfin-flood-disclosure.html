Buyers who saw flood-risk scores placed offers on homes with 57% lower flood risk.

Lily Katz · Daryl Fairweather · Sandoval-Olascoaga

Originally published - Redfin News ↗

A 3-month randomized controlled trial of 17.5 million Redfin.com users tested whether seeing property-level flood-risk scores changed buying behavior. Treated users who had been browsing high-flood-risk listings before the experiment placed offers on homes with 57% lower flood-risk scores than the control group. By week nine, treated users were also searching homes with 12.4% lower flood risk than controls.

The experiment partnered USC, MIT, and the National Bureau of Economic Research with Redfin to run the first large-scale RCT testing whether property-level climate-risk disclosure changes consumer behavior. Risk scores came from First Street Foundation's Flood Factor model and reflected the probability of flooding over a 30-year mortgage. Engagement concentrated in coastal and southern markets: Cape Coral, Florida (8.5% clickthrough on flood-risk content), Houston (8.1%), and Louisiana statewide (7.5%) led the country. The published version of this work, now under revision at the American Economic Review, extends the analysis with a longer panel and additional outcomes including transacted prices (Sandoval-Olascoaga et al., 2024).

Climate-risk data may start to have an even bigger impact on homebuyer decisions now that the housing market is slowing and tilting more in buyers' favor. Sandoval-Olascoaga, in the Redfin piece.
